Recognize a Stroke

The BE-FAST Method

BE-FAST is the most up-to-date method for identifying a stroke. It adds two critical early warning signs — Balance and Eyes — to the original FAST acronym.

B
Balance
Sudden loss of balance or coordination, dizziness, or difficulty walking that came on without warning.
E
Eyes
Sudden vision changes in one or both eyes — blurred, double, or complete loss of vision.
F
Face Drooping
Ask the person to smile. Is one side of their face drooping or numb? An uneven smile is a warning sign.
A
Arm Weakness
Ask them to raise both arms. Does one drift downward or feel weak and numb?
S
Speech Difficulty
Ask them to repeat a simple phrase. Is their speech slurred, strange, or impossible to understand?
T
Time to Call 911
If you observe ANY of these signs, call emergency services immediately. Note the time symptoms started.
⚡ Time is brain. For every minute a stroke goes untreated, roughly 1.9 million neurons die. The BE-FAST method catches up to 95% of strokes — acting on it immediately can save a life or prevent permanent disability.

Neuroscience

What happens in the brain during a stroke

A stroke is a neurological emergency. Understanding the biology explains why every second of treatment matters.

1

Blood supply is interrupted

The brain relies on a constant supply of oxygen-rich blood. During a stroke, a blood vessel either becomes blocked by a clot (ischemic) or bursts (hemorrhagic), cutting off blood flow to a region of the brain.

2

Neurons begin to die

Without oxygen, neurons — the brain's nerve cells — begin dying within minutes. The core area loses cells rapidly, while a surrounding "penumbra" zone is at risk but may still be saved with fast treatment.

3

Brain functions are disrupted

Different areas of the brain control different functions. Depending on where the stroke occurs, it can affect movement, speech, vision, balance, memory, or personality. The left hemisphere controls the right side of the body, and vice versa.

4

The damage cascade spreads

As cells die, they release toxic chemicals that damage neighboring neurons — a process called excitotoxicity. Inflammation and swelling follow, which can compress brain tissue and worsen outcomes significantly.

5

Treatment restores blood flow

For ischemic strokes, a clot-dissolving drug called tPA can restore blood flow if given within 4.5 hours of symptom onset. The faster treatment begins, the more brain tissue — and function — can be saved.

Types of Stroke

Not all strokes are the same

There are three main types of stroke, each with different causes, risks, and treatments.

Most Common

Ischemic Stroke

Caused by a blood clot blocking an artery supplying blood to the brain. The clot can form locally or travel from elsewhere in the body.

87%
of all strokes
Most Severe

Hemorrhagic Stroke

A blood vessel in the brain ruptures and bleeds into surrounding tissue. Often caused by high blood pressure or a weakened vessel wall such as an aneurysm.

13%
of all strokes
Warning Sign

TIA (Mini-Stroke)

Produces temporary stroke-like symptoms that resolve within 24 hours. It's a serious warning — never ignore it.

1 in 3
TIA patients have a full stroke within a year

Prevention

How to lower your stroke risk

Most strokes are preventable. These lifestyle habits make a significant difference in long-term brain health.

🩺

Control blood pressure

High blood pressure is the single biggest risk factor for stroke. Get it checked regularly and keep it below 120/80.

🥗

Eat a brain-healthy diet

A diet rich in vegetables, fruits, whole grains, and lean protein reduces inflammation and supports healthy blood vessels.

🏃

Exercise regularly

At least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week lowers blood pressure, reduces cholesterol, and improves cardiovascular health.

🚭

Don't smoke

Smoking doubles stroke risk by damaging blood vessel walls and increasing clot formation. Quitting reduces risk almost immediately.

💤

Sleep well

Poor sleep and sleep apnea are linked to higher stroke risk. Aim for 7–9 hours of quality sleep each night.

🧠

Manage stress

Chronic stress raises blood pressure and promotes inflammation. Mindfulness, exercise, and social connection all help reduce risk.
